About

Ideas

Feel

  • Conflict, whether physical or social, is driven by emotions, beliefs, and relationships. Mechanics for verbal and physical combat are similar and intertwined.
  • When conflicts happen, they are usually between two characters or two small groups of characters. The stakes of the conflict are mostly set up front, and there are consequences for not adhering to those terms when the fight is decided.
  • Push-and-pull for every action.
  • Elemental martial arts!
  • PbtA-ish, Burning Wheel Fight!-ish, Burning Wheel Duel of Wits-ish. Emphasis on the '-ish'

Player Safety

&&todo: update from god-hunter&&

We play RPGs with both friends and strangers, and with either we should promote the well-being of the people at the table over all else. There are times when the players or the GM may feel stressed or unsafe or otherwise may not be enjoying themselves. Use safety tools when playing God-Hunter as additional means and reminders to communicate with each other, and keep in mind that the use of safety tools is not an excuse to push each other's boundaries.

Here are brief introductions on a few to get you started, along with some further reading to find the tools that best suit your group.

Session Zero and Lines & Veils

The first time you play God-Hunter, discuss the tone and subjects of the game as a group. Introduce your safety tools. Create lines & veils. Choose your campaign troubles (see Portents).

Lines are hard limits. The subject of a line doesn't show up in the game at all, even in the background or in reference.

Veils are soft limits. These things can happen "off-screen"; they are spoken about or we "fade to black" when they occur, but we don't depict or roleplay them.

You can adjust your lines & veils as you play.

The X-Card

If you're playing in person, mark an index card with an 'X' and place it where everyone can reach it. During play, when someone taps the X-Card (or types an 'X' in chat while playing online) or asks to 'X-Card' something, we skip over or undo whatever's going on right now. No explanation needed. If the person that used the X-Card is comfortable discussing the content after the session, they may bring it up if they like.

Breaks and Checking In

After a set amount of time or when someone asks for a break, take one. Ask each other how you're doing regularly, including before and after the session.

Further Reading

Check out the TTRPG Safety Toolkit!

The TTRPG Safety Toolkit is a resource created by Kienna Shaw and Lauren Bryant-Monk. The TTRPG Safety Toolkit is a compilation of safety tools that have been designed by members of the tabletop roleplaying games community for use by players and GMs at the table. You can find it at bit.ly/ttrpgsafetytoolkit.

The Game

Game is in two phases: free-play and conflicts.

Free-Play

Attributes in Free-Play

Your attributes determine how many dice you roll when using moves in free-play.

  • Sky—perception, detachment from your own desires and fears
  • Wind—grace in movement and speech
  • Fire—passion and all-out offense
  • Water—adaptability and understanding of others
  • Earth—endurance and strength in body and mind

Obstacles & Success

  • When you attempt something in free-play, you roll against an obstacle. This is the number of successes (rolls of 4+) you need to fully succeed.
    • Obstacle 1: A routine task.
    • Obstacle 2: A difficult task.
    • Obstacle 3: A challenging task.
    • Obstacle 4: An impossible task.
  • If you meet or exceed the obstacle, you succeed.
  • If you get at least one success but fall short of the obstacle, another player chooses one:
    • You don't succeed.
    • You succeed, but with additional costs or consequences.
  • If you don't meet the obstacle, you don't succeed and must deal with additional costs or consequences.
Conflicts

When a fight or debate is nigh, we switch to the conflict phase.

Stakes & Manner

All players must agree what the resolution of this conflict will decide. Each side should state what they'll get if they win. All players must agree that stakes are plausible within the fiction.

  • If the winning side wins with no wounds, they owe no concessions to the losing side.
  • If the winning side wins with 1 or 2 wounds, they owe a minor concession.
  • If the winning side wins with 3 or more wounds, they owe a major concession.

The Lord of Flames and the Hero are fighting for the fate of Capital City. If the Lord of Flames wins, the he destroys Capital City utterly and leaves the Hero for dead. If the Hero wins, Capital City is unharmed and the Lord of Flames is captured.

When the battle is said and done, the Hero is victorious, but they took 3 wounds in the process, thus they owe the Lord of Flames a major concession. Capital City is safe for now, but the Lord of Flames slips away.

The Princess and the Monk are debating who should have the right to explore the Ancestral Shrine recently uncovered.

All players must agree on whether the conflict is violent. If not, you can't use any moves marked as violent until the Escalate move is used. Similarly, a move marked as nonviolent can't be used in a violent conflict. All other moves may be used in both violent and nonviolent conflicts. &&How should deescalating work?&&

The Build-Up

How you feel going into a fight or argument affects your starting pool.

You have 3 build-ups. Spend 1 each to describe a scene before the fight, a memory you recall, or a belief you're focusing on. For each, sow / / / —OR—reap and sow —OR—reset a bond. Some examples:

  • Do you recall long hours of training where you honed rhetoric or mastered obstacle courses? Sow a Wind.
  • Do your friends cheer your name from the stands, reminding you what you're fighting for? You've got a Fire.
  • Did you spend time researching your foes, preparing yourself for anything they might throw at you? Take a Water.
  • Does your mentor take you aside to reassure you? You've got an Earth to work with.
  • Did you take the time to meditate on everything you've learned? Reap Any element and sow one Sky, the element needed to create the most powerful effects.

The Conflict

  • You accrue elemental resources (5 elements: sky, wind, fire, water, earth), using certain moves to add them to your pool (sowing)
  • You spend those resources with other moves (reaping)
  • Your attributes (sky, wind, fire, water, earth) determine how many of a particular element you can have in your pool.
  • If you would ever sow more of an element than you can hold in your pool, you are unbalanced. Your opponent may make an extra offensive move immediately, and you lose any excess elements.

Turns, Attacking & Defending

&&Definitely turn-based, details are iffy.&&

  • You take turns in the fight. Some moves give you initiative, letting you take another turn right after.
  • Starting initiative is determined by rolling your + attributes. Reroll ties.
  • When it's your turn, you make a move.
  • If you make move with the Defend tag on your turn, it adds bonus dice to your next defense roll and usually sows an element, too. These extra dice are lost after you're attacked or at the start of your next turn.
  • If you make a move with the Attack tag, declare you are doing so and which opponent(s) it will affect, keeping which attack you're using a secret.
    • Your opponent chooses a move with the Defend tag in secret. The element of a defense is always determined by this move.
    • You both reveal your moves simultaneously and resolve any effects.
    • You roll dice equal to the number next to the Attack tag. Each roll of 4+ is a success.
    • Your opponent rolls dice equal to the number next to the Defend tag. Each roll of 4+ is a success.
    • For each success you have above your opponent, you deal them a wound. Your opponent records these and their fictional nature.

  • Each player chooses 3 cards (duplicates ok) to play facedown
  • Each volley, each player reveals their next card, resolving effects simultaneously

Roleplay Die

  • Each time cards are revealed, players describe what they're doing. If their roleplay fits the descriptions and themes in their cards, they take +1d to their roll. You don't need to be master orator or duelist yourself--if the you make a sincere and deliberate attempt, you get the die.

Wounds

  • Wounds reduce each of the maximum number of elements in your pool. Example:
    • The highest value for any stat is 5.
    • Your highest stat is 3. Your next-highest stat is 2.
    • Your first wound you take lowers your max from 5 to 4, which doesn't affect any of your stats.
    • Your second wound you take lowers your max from 4 to 3, which doesn't affect any of your stats.
    • Your third wound lowers your max from 3 to 2. You can only hold 2 instead of 3, and if you had 3 in your pool already, you lose one.
    • Your fourth wound lowers your max from 2 to 1. You can only hold 1 and 1 now. If you had 2 , lose 1 . If you had 2 , lose 1 .
    • Your fifth wound knocks you out of the conflict.
  • Each time you take a wound, record its nature. Burns? Damaged reputation? Bruised ribs? Bruised ego?
  • With 1 or 2 wounds, you recover quickly on your own.
  • With 3 or 4 wounds,
  • With 5 wounds, you're unconscious. Someone must come to your aid. Who is it?

Conflict Moves

  • Use moves to sow elements, storing them in your pool. The number next to the element icon tells you how many of that element you gain. If the number is a variable like 'x', the move's description will tell you what to do with it.
  • The maximum you can sow of a particular element is given by your stats.
  • Use moves to reap elements, spending them from your pool. The number next to the element icon tells you spend. If the number is a variable like 'x', you choose how many of that element to reap unless the move's description says otherwise.

See moves.

Misc

  • An element symbol without a number next to it means 1 of that element.
  • If you ever need to roll 0 or negative dice, roll 2d6 and take the lowest instead.
  • If after applying modifiers you ever need to sow or reap a negative amount, sow or reap 0 instead.
  • If a move both sows and reaps, that reap cost can't be paid by an elements sown by that move that turn.
  • represents any element. If a move tells you to sow , choose any element to add to your pool. If a move tells you to reap , pick any element from your pool to spend.

Changelog

This early in the game's development, changes are going to be wild and frequent, and some may go undocumented. Versioning should goes as follows: "Alpha/Beta/Release.Major.Minor"

Alpha/Beta/Release. Alpha: feature incomplete. Beta: mostly feature complete, polishing. Release: near publication-ready.

Major. Breaking changes or large content. Usually a public release.

Minor. Tweaks. Usually a private release.

Copied from daeva page:

Like many magical entities, they inflict supernatural awe and terror. Anyone without a Golden Arrow must make a fortune roll using their quality or Tier when coming in direct contact with a daeva. If their quality or Tier is lower than the daeva's, they take -1 result level (1-3 minimum). People with a Golden Arrow take +1 result level.

  • Critical: They're immune to this entity's aura for this scene.
  • 6: They can bite back their fear long enough to act.
  • 4-5: They hesitate. Their effect is reduced or they're left open for attack.
  • 1-3: They stand and drool or run screaming.

PCs may resist this aura with Resolve or use special armor (if applicable) as normal. If they choose not to, they roll using the caravan's Tier.
